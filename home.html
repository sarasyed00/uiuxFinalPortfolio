Objective

Design and implement a simple website using React that lists items and has the functionality to filter, sort, and aggregate them.

Overview

In this project, I used CSS, Javascript, and React to develop a food recipe site! This was my first project heavily using Javascript and React. While the site is minimalistic, it has the functionality to filter and sort recipes by features like difficulty level and time. You can also add recipes you are interested in to "Your Menu".

Goal

My goal was to create a simple and intuitive application that could be applicable to many sites like cooking blogs. By being able to sort by meal types and difficulty levels, users could find recipes that were appropriate for them.

Design

I tried to implement usability principles that would allow the application to be intuitive. For one, I made sure to keep the site minamalistic and only include the information that was needed. I included simple design elements to allow users to understand when buttons or features were activated vs deactivated. For example, when a user adds a recipe to their Menu, the button not only changes its wording but also fills in the heart. I also included simple additions like alt tags to improve accessibilty.

Structure

For more information checkout the application's README

This application has a very simple design structure. The App.js file contains RecipeList, a list of all the recipe items and their information. This information is passed into a FilterList component (FilterList.js). FilterList.js includes the functionality for filtering and sorting the RecipeList. FilterList.js instantiates a NavBar component from NavBar.js, which displays the react-bootstrap navigation bars. The recipe items themselves are laid out in the DisplayItem component (from DisplayItem.js) and displayed in the DisplayList component from (DisplayList.js). Filter.js instantiates this DisplayList component. Filter.js also includes the functionality for aggregating items in the DisplayMenu component, which it instantiates from DisplayMenu.js.

Conclusion

I was able to create this project and fulfill my original goal. While it was challenging to learn to use a different language and new libraries, I was happy with the final outcome and felt that I was able to take advantage of their respective features. While my final application was simplistic and highly generalized, it was fully functional and could be easily customized.

Objective

Redesign a local company's website to improve aspects of usability, accessibility, and equitability.

The Company

Bagel Gourmet Cafe (BGC), my favorite Providence bagel shop! Check out BGC's original website: BGC's site.
Webpage: I focused specifically on redesigning their Menu page. In order to construct my design improvements, I first analyzed their existing site on factors of usability, learnability, accessibility, and memorability. Here are some points of improvement I noted:

usability, learnability, memorability:

  • Small text in the navigation bar
  • Location of navigation bar changes (moves from top to the left side when off home page)
  • Inconsistent use of colors (black, blue, various shades of red)
  • Significance of underlined text is not uniform (in some it means its a link, in others its to denote importance)
  • The word "we" appears on the Bagel gourmet cafe logo when on any page besides the home page
  • No readjustments or responsive design to interfaces of different sizes

accessibility:

  • Missing language tag
  • Missing page title
  • Missing alt tags for photos
  • Contrast errors (between text and background)
  • Very small text warnings

BGC's current site could use some major redesign to improve aspects of accesibility. The simple addition of alt tags, larger font size, and greater contrasting colors will make the site usable by a broader range of people.

Objective

Work in a group of three to design an interface for an emerging startup. Sketch ideas of the interface, create an interactive high-fidelity prototype, and conduct user testing on this final prototype.


The Startup

Drapr builds software to help online shoppers see what their clothing looks like. The company's online try-on widget helps shoppers get a better idea of how a shirt or other clothing item fits on a customizable 3D mannequin that users can adjust to fit their body type, height and weight.

Type of Interface: Desktop website

Users: This website is marketing DRAPR to online shopping retailers who want to make it easy for their customers to virtually try on clothes. DRAPR's try-on widget could reduce return rate and prevent dissatisfied customers. It will be especially helpful for customers who have body shapes or are sizes that differ from the typical model. More people are buying clothing online due to the pandemic, and do not have the option to try on clothes in stores, making DRAPR a timely alternative.

Critical Feedback


We had a mockup crit, wherein we received feedback on our design from an industry professional and our classmates. These are some of their suggestions, which we implemented below.

  • No back button on checkout page
  • Can't see package prices on main page
  • Split the checkout page into login and payment
  • Hard to find checkout
  • No affordances to indicate that page scrolls
  • Match nav headers to the content headings
  • "Click to Customize" is positioned so it points to nothing
  • Button alignment should be consistent between pages (center all)

User Testing

Using the critical feedback, we created another iteration of our prototype, which we sent our prototype to usertesting.com and gave 3 users the following instructions:

Scenario: You are an employee for a clothing company. You are looking for additional features to add to your website to improve the shopping experience. Drapr is a company that offers virtual "try-it-on" software that can be embedded into websites. You will be evaluating the different plans that Drapr offers, and purchasing a plan for your clothing company.

Overall Task: Navigate the website, and purchase a plan. Once the plan is purchased, send a message to the PR team to get in touch with the company.

Subtasks:

  1. Scroll down the website, taking note of the main features offered by the product
  2. Select the "Basic Plan" from the plans offered
  3. "Create an Account" to use for purchasing the plan
  4. Enter billing details and confirm the purchase
  5. Return to the homepage
  6. Send a brief message to the PR team using the “Contact Us” feature
  7. Return to the homepage

Post-Task Questions:

  1. Did you face any challenges while using the website? If so, what were they?
  2. On a scale of 1-10, how intuitive do you find the website design?
  3. Are there any additional features that you would have liked on the website?
  4. Is there anything else you would like to tell us/any additional comments you have?

Overview

Summary of Results

Overall, we were fairly pleased with the results we obtained from the user testing experiments. Here are some of our findings:

  • Navigating through the homepage was generally in line with expectations. We were surprised to find that one of the users still had a bit of difficulty figuring out that the website scrolled (even with affordances such as peaking and a small arrow on the bottom of the page)
  • Users generally remembered where to go for latter subtasks (Contact Us, etc…). However, this relied on recall rather than intuition (the users specifically stated that they "remembered where on the page this form was")
  • Some users asked for a bit more specificity on the "try-it-on" widget
  • When selecting the plans, users were generally able to navigate without errors. One user suggested creating more contrasting color palettes in the actual choice icons for the different plans
  • Users were dissatisfied with the vagueness of the landing page after sending a message to the PR team. They would have preferred to know an exact timeframe for receiving a response
  • After the users figured out the scrolling mechanism, they made no errors on any of the subsequent tasks. We received feedback from those users that the navigation was simple and easy to use

Interface Changes

  • Our biggest change would be to make the affordances even more clear for scrolling. Not understanding the scroll made the website nearly completely unusable, so perhaps a scrolling bar on the right of the page or something similar would ensure this mistake is not made.
  • We would add a bit more detail and information on the landing pages for the "checkout" and "contact us" buttons. This would reassure customers that they performed the correct tasks.
  • We might change the color scheme slightly on the icons for the three different plans. As is, it may be a bit difficult for customers to intuitively and immediately grasp the idea that the plans are radically different given the similarity in color schemes.
  • We might make fewer assumptions on familiarity with the exact measurements required for using the "try-it-on" widget and perhaps add a "help" icon that walks users through the steps of taking the necessary steps to use the widget.
  • Users generally relied on recall rather than intuition for navigating to specific elements on the scrollable home page. In the future we would add a clickable navigation bar on the top of the page that takes users directly to different sections of the page.
    • Note: We considered implementing this feature in our original prototype, but unfortunately Figma did not have this functionality. Given its importance, we would definitely implement this in a real website.

Conclusion

Lessons Learned: This project taught us that there are often hidden usability issues in a design that can only be found with outside feedback. Both the critique and the user testing proved invaluable for exposing usability issues. We learned that it's important to consult a wide variety of opinions, because demographics like age can affect how intuitive users find an interface. I additionally, was able to put into practice soft skills liek critiquing and working in teams, and hard skills like user testing, wireframing, HiFi prototyping, and iterative design.

Objective

Develop personas and use storyboarding to identify design issues of an everyday object or interface

The Object

Are gas pumps intuitive and designed for everyone to use?

First, let’s take a look at a typical modern gas pump:

The gas pump is used to refuel a car. It is a large, interactive machine, with a gas nozzle (the part that is inserted into the car) and a gas hose which is used to pump fuel from the underground gasoline storage tanks. When the 'operating lever' is held back or clamped, it releases gasoline.

Overview

Interviews

Next, I interviewed three users, two of whom had plenty of previous experience pumping their own gas, and one who did not. Here were the questions I asked:

  1. How frequently do you use the gas pump? (Weekly? Monthly? Seasonally?)
  2. When was the first time you pumped your own gas?
  3. Did you need someone to teach you or was it intuitive?
  4. When you pump gas do you fill your entire tank?
  5. Have any aspects of the gas pump been challenging for you to use?
  6. Have you been to a gas station whose pump design was unique? Were the features easier to use?
    7.

Personas

From my interviews, I developed two personas:

College Casey represents the new driver. She recently learned how to use the gas pump machine, but rarely does so alone. She has a tight budget and is sometimes afraid to ask for help. She is unaware of the differences in the types of gas. She is often unsure if she securely placed the nozzle into the car, and does not know how to identify when her car tank has been filled all the way.

Senior Sylvie represents the experienced driver. She has frequently pumped gas but still faces difficulties with some of the bulky physical components of the gas pump and nozzle. She is patient and takes her time. She finds the weight and size of the gas nozzle to be straining. She dislikes when gas pumps lack a 'hold open latch' as she does not like to have to hold the 'operating lever' while she fills her entire tank. She sometimes has difficulty seeing the instruction screen when it is poorly lit.

Conclusion

From interviewing and creating personas, I was better able to understand the type of users who use the gas pump, ranging from novices to frequent users, from young to old.

From understanding users' mindsets, many key design flaws of the gas pump and nozzle were revealed: from the weight and size of the gas nozzle, to the illumination of the instruction screen, to the lack of indication when the nozzle is securely placed in the car. These factors could be used to redesign the gas pump to make it more intuitive and accessible.

This project revealed to me how even everyday items, of whom's design has probably been reiterated on many times before, can still be reevaluated and improved. By keeping in mind extreme users, we can identify space of improvement for these objects in areas such as accessibilty and inclusivity.
